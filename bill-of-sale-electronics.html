// The dispute this prevents

"IT ARRIVED BROKEN — AND THE SERIAL'S WRONG."

Used electronics are the easiest category to dispute and the hardest to defend. A buyer claims the screen was cracked on arrival, that the battery is shot, that the IMEI is blacklisted, or that the serial doesn't match what you listed — opening the door to a swap, where they return a broken unit in place of the working one you sent. On a marketplace, the seller almost always loses these without independent, dated proof of what shipped.

Without a sealed record
A listing photo with no provable date. A serial typed into a chat. No record of the unit powering on before it shipped. When the buyer claims a swap or a fault, you can't prove which device you actually sent.
With a SENT. bill of sale
A dated, cryptographically sealed record binding the serial, IMEI, working condition and the actual photos to this exact unit — created before it shipped and impossible to backdate. A return swap can't pass for the device you sold.

// Questions

ELECTRONICS SELLERS ASK

Is this a legal document?
It's cryptographic proof of a transaction and its agreed terms — what was sold, by whom, to whom, when, and in what condition. It is not a legal title transfer and not legal advice. It's independent, tamper-evident evidence you can show a buyer, a marketplace, or a payment processor.
Why record the serial number or IMEI?
The serial or IMEI binds the record to the exact unit you sold. If a buyer claims you shipped a different device, or accuses you of selling a blacklisted unit, the sealed serial and your dated photos show precisely what left your hands.
How does it help with an "arrived broken" claim?
The record is sealed before you ship and timestamped cryptographically, so it can't be backdated. You have a dated record of the working condition, accessories and photos you both agreed to at the point of sale.
